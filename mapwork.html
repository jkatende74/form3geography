Mapwork

Section 1: The Fundamentals of Maps

Mapwork is the study and practice of interpreting and using maps. A map is a two-dimensional, scaled representation of the Earth's surface, or a part of it, showing physical and human-made features. For the KCSE syllabus, the primary focus is on topographical maps, which are detailed maps showing landforms (relief), drainage, vegetation, and cultural (human) features.

1.1 Marginal Information: The Map's Metadata

Before reading a map, one must understand its marginal information—the details provided in the map's border or "margin". These essentials provide context and are crucial for accurate interpretation.

  • Title and Sheet Name: Tells you the specific area the map covers (e.g., "EAST AFRICA 1:50,000 (KENYA)," with a sheet name like "KARATINA").
  • Sheet Number and Series: A unique identifier for the map sheet (e.g., Sheet 121/3).
  • Publisher and Copyright Date: Indicates who created the map (e.g., Survey of Kenya) and when, which helps in assessing its currency.
  • Scale: Shows the relationship between a distance on the map and the corresponding distance on the ground.
  • Key (or Legend): Explains the meaning of all the conventional signs and symbols used on the map.
  • Directional Information: Includes details on True North, Grid North, and Magnetic North, along with the magnetic variation.
  • Contour Interval: States the vertical distance between adjacent contour lines.

Section 2: Position, Direction, and Location

The KCSE syllabus requires proficiency in locating features using various methods, including direction and grid references.

2.1 Direction and Bearing

  • Direction: The position of one place relative to another, often expressed using the 16 cardinal points of a compass (N, NNE, NE, ENE, E, etc.).
  • Bearing: A more precise measurement of direction given as an angle in degrees, measured clockwise from North.

Types of North

  • True North: The direction to the Earth's geographic North Pole.
  • Grid North: The direction of the vertical grid lines (Eastings) on a map. It's often assumed to be the same as True North in exercises.
  • Magnetic North: The direction a compass needle points. It changes over time.

Measuring Bearing

  1. Identify the starting point ("from") and the destination point.
  2. Draw a straight line connecting the two points.
  3. At the starting point, draw a North-South line parallel to the grid lines.
  4. Place a protractor with its baseline along the North-South line and its center at the starting point.
  5. Measure the angle clockwise from the North line to the line connecting the two points. This angle is the bearing, expressed in three figures (e.g., 045°, 259°).

2.2 The Grid Reference System

This system allows for the precise location of any feature on a topographical map using a grid of numbered horizontal and vertical lines. The rule is to "Read Right, then Up".

Four-Figure Grid Reference

Used to locate a grid square.

  1. Read the number of the Easting that forms the left side of the square.
  2. Read the number of the Northing that forms the bottom side of the square.
  3. Example: A square bounded by Easting `34` and Northing `56` has a grid reference of `3456`.

Six-Figure Grid Reference

Used to pinpoint a specific feature *within* a grid square.

  1. Find the four-figure reference for the grid square.
  2. Mentally divide the square into 10 smaller vertical divisions. Estimate how many tenths "right" from the Easting the feature is. This is the 3rd digit.
  3. Mentally divide the square into 10 smaller horizontal divisions. Estimate how many tenths "up" from the Northing the feature is. This is the 6th digit.
  4. Example: A school in grid square `3456`, located 7-tenths to the east and 2-tenths to the north, has a six-figure reference of `347562`.

Section 3: Scale, Distance, and Area

3.1 Types of Scale

  • Statement Scale: Expresses the relationship in words (e.g., "One centimetre represents half a kilometre").
  • Representative Fraction (RF) or Ratio Scale: A ratio where the numerator (always 1) represents map distance and the denominator represents ground distance in the same units (e.g., 1:50,000 means 1 cm on the map is 50,000 cm on the ground).
  • Linear Scale: A graduated line or bar for direct measurement of distance on the map.

Scale Conversion (1:50,000 to Statement)

Since 100,000 cm = 1 km, then $50,000 \text{ cm} = 0.5 \text{ km}$. Therefore, the statement scale is "1 cm represents 0.5 km".

3.2 Measuring Distance

  • Straight-Line Distance: Measure with a ruler and convert using the scale. (e.g., 8 cm on a 1:50,000 map = $8 \times 0.5 \text{ km} = 4 \text{ km}$).
  • Curved-Line Distance: Use the edge of a piece of paper, pivoting along the curve. Measure the marked length on the paper and convert using the scale.

3.3 Calculating Area

  • Regular Shapes: Approximate as a rectangle, measure map dimensions, convert to ground dimensions, then use Area = Length × Width.
  • Irregular Shapes (Grid Square Method):
    1. Count the number of **full squares** covered by the feature.
    2. Count the **partially covered squares** and divide by two to get an estimate of equivalent full squares.
    3. Add the full and partial counts to get the total number of squares.
    4. Multiply the total squares by the area of one grid square (e.g., on a 1:50,000 map, one square is 1 km²).

Section 4: Relief Representation and Interpretation

4.1 Methods of Representing Relief

  • Contours: Brown lines connecting places of equal height. The **Contour Interval** is the vertical distance between them.
  • Spot Heights: Dots with a height value.
  • Trigonometrical Stations: Precisely calculated points marked with a triangle.
  • Hill Shading: Shading to depict shadows cast by high ground.
  • Layer Tinting: Using different colors for different elevation bands.

4.2 Interpreting Landforms from Contours

Slope

  • Gentle Slope: Widely spaced contours.
  • Steep Slope: Closely packed contours.
  • Concave Slope: Widely spaced at top, close at bottom.
  • Convex Slope: Close at top, widely spaced at bottom.

Features

  • Valley: V-shaped contours pointing to higher ground.
  • Spur: V or U-shaped contours pointing to lower ground.
  • Ridge: A long, narrow stretch of highland.
  • Escarpment: A long, steep slope with a gentle slope on the other side.
  • Col/Saddle: A dip or low point between two peaks.

Section 5: Advanced Mapwork Skills

5.1 Drawing a Cross-Section (Profile)

A cross-section shows the side-view of the relief along a specific line.

  1. Identify the two endpoints on the map and join them with a straight line.
  2. Use a straight-edged piece of paper, place it along the line, and mark where each contour crosses it, noting the height.
  3. On graph paper, draw a horizontal axis (same scale as the map) and a vertical axis (with a suitable scale for heights).
  4. Project the marked points from your paper edge upwards to their corresponding height on the graph.
  5. Join the plotted points with a smooth curve. Add a title and label all axes.

5.2 Vertical Exaggeration (VE)

VE shows how many times the vertical scale is larger than the horizontal scale. Formula: $VE = \frac{\text{Denominator of HS}}{\text{Denominator of VS}}$.

Example: If HS is 1:50,000 and VS is 1cm to 100m (1:10,000), then $VE = \frac{50,000}{10,000} = 5$. The relief is exaggerated 5 times.

5.3 Calculating Gradient

Gradient is the steepness of a slope. Formula: $\text{Gradient} = \frac{\text{Vertical Interval (VI)}}{\text{Horizontal Equivalent (HE)}}$.

  1. Find the difference in height between the two points (VI).
  2. Find the horizontal ground distance between the points (HE).
  3. Ensure VI and HE are in the same units.
  4. Calculate the gradient and express it as a ratio (e.g., 1:10).

5.4 Determining Intervisibility

Intervisibility is whether one point on a map can be seen from another.

  • Cross-Section Method: Draw a cross-section. If a straight line connecting the two points on the profile is not obstructed by the land, they are intervisible.
  • Contour Method: Examine the contour lines. If there is higher ground between the two points, they are not intervisible.

Section 6: Integrated Map Interpretation

This involves reading the physical and human information together to understand the geography of the area.

  • Relief: Describe the overall landscape (mountainous, hilly, plain), highest/lowest points, and specific landforms.
  • Drainage: Identify rivers, lakes, swamps, and their patterns (dendritic, radial, trellis).
  • Vegetation: Describe the type and distribution of natural vegetation and its relation to relief and drainage.
  • Human Activities:
    • Settlement: Note patterns (nucleated, linear, dispersed) and influencing factors.
    • Economic Activities: Look for evidence of farming, mining, forestry, trade, etc.
    • Transport: Identify the network of roads, railways, and how it relates to settlements.
  • Climate and Soils: These can be inferred from vegetation, crop types, and drainage features.

113. Identify and describe three common drainage patterns.

1. **Dendritic:** Tree-like pattern, tributaries join the main river at acute angles. 2. **Trellis:** Tributaries join main rivers at right angles. 3. **Radial:** Rivers flow outwards from a central high point.

115. Why is information about magnetic variation included in map marginal information?

Because Magnetic North changes over time, the variation is crucial for accurate navigation using a compass at the time the map is used.

116. When describing drainage, what should you look for to infer the climate of an area?

The presence of permanent rivers (suggesting a wet climate) versus seasonal or intermittent rivers (suggesting a climate with dry seasons or low rainfall).

117. What is the ultimate goal of integrated map interpretation?

To read the physical and human information together to understand the geography of the area and the relationship between different features.

118. What is a key precaution when describing features on a map?

Avoid definite words; instead, use phrases like "suggests that," "possibly," or "likely indication of," and support observations with multiple pieces of evidence.

119. How can you infer soil type from map evidence?

By looking at drainage (intermittent rivers suggest porous soils, swamps suggest waterlogged soils) and crop types (tea/coffee suggest fertile volcanic soils).
